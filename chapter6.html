Seekho IPC - Chapter VI

Chapter VI: Offences against the State (Sections 121 to 130)

Section 121: Waging, or Attempting to Wage War, or Abetting Waging of War, Against the Government of India

Description

This section deals with the criminal offenses related to waging or attempting to wage war against the Government of India. It includes acts of abetting such actions and outlines the severe penalties for engaging in these activities.

Example 1

An individual involved in planning a terrorist attack against the Indian government or its officials would be charged under this section.

Example 2

Someone who provides material support or recruits individuals for a group aiming to overthrow the Indian government could be prosecuted under this section.

Section 121A: Conspiracy to Commit Offences Punishable by Section 121

Description

This section addresses the crime of conspiring to commit offenses that fall under Section 121. It outlines the penalties for individuals involved in planning or conspiring to wage war against the Government of India.

Example 1

A group of individuals planning an insurgent campaign against the government would be prosecuted under this section if their conspiracy involves actions described in Section 121.

Example 2

Anyone caught plotting to assist a foreign power in waging war against India could face charges under this section.

Section 122: Collecting Arms, etc., with Intention of Waging War Against the Government of India

Description

This section covers the offense of collecting arms, ammunition, or other resources with the intent to wage war against the Government of India. It outlines the legal repercussions for such actions.

Example 1

Individuals found stockpiling weapons with plans to initiate a rebellion against the government would be charged under this section.

Example 2

Acquiring explosives and military gear to support an insurgency would be prosecutable under this section.

Section 123: Concealing with Intent to Facilitate Design to Wage War

Description

This section addresses the crime of concealing information or actions that facilitate plans to wage war against the Government of India. It describes the penalties for individuals who intentionally hide their involvement in such designs.

Example 1

A person hiding documents or communication related to an upcoming attack on the government would be charged under this section.

Example 2

Concealing the identity of individuals involved in planning a coup against the government would also fall under this section.

Section 124A: Sedition

Description

This section deals with sedition, defined as acts or speech intended to incite discontent or rebellion against the Government of India. It outlines the legal consequences for those found guilty of such acts.

Example 1

Publishing inflammatory material that incites violence or rebellion against the government would be prosecuted under this section.

Example 2

Speech or demonstrations that encourage people to overthrow the government would be considered seditious under this section.

Section 125: Waging War Against Any Asiatic Power in Alliance with the Government of India

Description

This section covers the crime of waging war against an Asiatic power that is allied with the Government of India. It outlines the consequences for such acts of aggression against allied states.

Example 1

An individual or group attacking an ally of India, such as a neighboring nation with a defense pact, would be prosecuted under this section.

Example 2

A conspiracy to sabotage or harm an ally's military interests in support of an enemy would fall under this section.

Section 126: Committing Depredation on Territories of Power at Peace with the Government of India

Description

This section addresses the crime of committing depredations (such as raids or attacks) on territories of a power that is at peace with the Government of India. It specifies the legal penalties for such acts.

Example 1

Attacking or looting property in a nation that has peaceful relations with India would be covered under this section.

Example 2

Cross-border raids on peaceful neighboring countries to steal resources would be prosecuted under this section.

Section 127: Receiving Property Taken by War on Depredation Mentioned in Sections 125 and 126

Description

This section deals with the offense of receiving property that was taken through acts of war or depredation as described in Sections 125 and 126. It outlines the penalties for individuals involved in such actions.

Example 1

Acquiring goods or valuables looted from a friendly nation during an unlawful raid would be covered under this section.

Example 2

Receiving stolen items from a conflict or raid that targeted a peaceful neighboring state would also fall under this section.

Section 128: Public Servant Voluntarily Allowing Prisoner of State or War to Escape

Description

This section addresses the crime of a public servant voluntarily permitting a prisoner of state or war to escape. It describes the legal consequences for such negligence or intentional misconduct.

Example 1

A jailor deliberately allowing a high-profile prisoner of war to escape would be charged under this section.

Example 2

A public servant intentionally not securing the escape of a state prisoner during transport would also fall under this section.

Section 129: Public Servant Negligently Suffering Such Prisoner to Escape

Description

This section deals with the offense of a public servant negligently allowing a prisoner of state or war to escape. It describes the legal repercussions for failing to take necessary precautions to prevent such escapes.

Example 1

A guard failing to properly lock the cell of a high-security prisoner, resulting in an escape, would be charged under this section.

Example 2

A public official who neglects to follow security protocols for a prisoner transfer, leading to an escape, would also be prosecuted under this section.

Section 130: Aiding Escape of, Rescuing or Harbouring Such Prisoner

Description

This section covers the crime of aiding the escape of, rescuing, or harboring a prisoner of state or war. It specifies the penalties for those who assist such prisoners in evading capture.

Example 1

Helping a prisoner of war to escape and providing them with shelter would be prosecuted under this section.

Example 2

Assisting in the rescue of a high-profile state prisoner and concealing their location from authorities would also fall under this section.

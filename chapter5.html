Seekho IPC - Chapter V

Chapter V: Abetment (Sections 107 to 120)

Section 107: Abetment of a Thing

Description

This section defines abetment as the act of aiding, instigating, or encouraging another person to commit an offense. It outlines what constitutes abetment and the circumstances under which a person can be considered an abettor.

Example 1

If someone encourages another person to commit theft, they are considered to be abetting the theft under this section.

Example 2

Providing tools or resources to someone with the intention of helping them commit a crime also constitutes abetment.

Section 108: Abettor

Description

This section defines who can be considered an abettor, including those who knowingly assist, encourage, or facilitate the commission of an offense. It specifies the roles and responsibilities of an abettor in the context of criminal acts.

Example 1

A person who provides information or a plan to facilitate a crime is considered an abettor if their actions directly aid in the commission of the offense.

Example 2

An individual who helps conceal the identity of a person committing a crime can also be considered an abettor if their assistance is crucial to the crime being carried out.

Section 108A: Abetment in India of Offences Outside India

Description

This section addresses the abetment of offenses committed outside India by individuals within the country. It outlines how Indian law applies to those who aid, abet, or assist in the commission of offenses beyond Indian borders.

Example 1

If an individual in India provides resources or plans to someone committing a crime abroad, they may be held liable under this section for their role in the abetment of that foreign offense.

Example 2

Facilitating illegal activities such as money laundering for crimes committed outside India may result in prosecution under this section for abetment.

Section 110: Punishment of Abetment if Person Abetted Does Act with Different Intention from that of Abettor

Description

This section provides guidelines on the punishment for abetment when the person who was abetted acts with a different intention from that of the abettor. It addresses the legal consequences when the outcomes differ from the abettor's original intent.

Example 1

If someone abets another to commit a robbery with the intention of obtaining money, but the act results in a different crime like assault, the abettor’s punishment may be adjusted based on this section.

Example 2

If an abettor’s intended crime was theft, but the person abetted ends up committing arson, the abettor's punishment may be determined according to the unintended consequences.

Section 111: Liability of Abettor When One Act Abetted and Different Act Done

Description

This section outlines the liability of an abettor when the act committed differs from the one that was abetted. It clarifies the extent to which the abettor can be held responsible for the act that was actually carried out.

Example 1

If an abettor plans and encourages a theft but the person abetted ends up committing a robbery, the abettor may still be held liable depending on the circumstances and intent.

Example 2

An abettor who aids a person to commit one specific crime but the person ends up committing a different, albeit related, crime, may still face liability for the act actually committed.

Section 112: Abettor When Liable to Cumulative Punishment for Act Abetted and for Act Done

Description

This section specifies the circumstances under which an abettor can be subjected to cumulative punishment for both the act abetted and the actual act committed. It addresses cases where both the intended and actual acts are criminal in nature.

Example 1

If an abettor encourages and facilitates a crime and the actual crime committed is of a more severe nature, the abettor may face cumulative punishment for both the abetment and the committed crime.

Example 2

In a case where an abettor’s encouragement leads to a different, more serious crime, they may be punished for both their role in abetting and the actual crime carried out by the perpetrator.

Section 113: Liability of Abettor for an Effect Caused by the Act Abetted Different from that Intended by the Abettor

Description

This section deals with the liability of an abettor when the effect of the act committed differs from what was originally intended by the abettor. It outlines how the abettor is held accountable for unintended consequences of their actions.

Example 1

If an abettor encourages someone to commit a theft, but the act results in a murder, the abettor may be held liable for the murder if it is a foreseeable consequence of the abetted act.

Example 2

An abettor who plans a robbery but the actual crime committed is a violent assault could face liability for the assault if it was a foreseeable outcome of the robbery.

Section 114: Abettor Present When Offence is Committed

Description

This section addresses the situation where an abettor is present at the scene when the offense is committed. It defines the legal implications and responsibilities of an abettor who witnesses the crime they have abetted.

Example 1

If an abettor is present during the commission of a robbery they facilitated, their presence could be used as evidence of their involvement and intent.

Example 2

An abettor who remains at the crime scene while a crime they helped orchestrate is taking place may be held accountable for the crime itself, depending on their actions and involvement.

Section 115: Abetment of Offence Punishable with Death or Imprisonment for Life - If Offence Not Committed

Description

This section covers the abetment of offenses punishable with death or imprisonment for life, even if the actual offense is not committed. It specifies the penalties for abetment in such serious cases.

Example 1

If an abettor plans a murder that is ultimately thwarted, the abettor may still be liable for abetment, with penalties reflective of the seriousness of the intended crime.

Example 2

Abetting a crime punishable by life imprisonment, such as an assassination, even if the assassination does not occur, can still lead to severe penalties for the abettor.

Section 116: Abetment of Offence Punishable with Imprisonment - If Offence Not Committed

Description

This section deals with the abetment of offenses punishable with imprisonment, even if the actual offense is not carried out. It outlines the legal repercussions for abetting such crimes.

Example 1

An abettor who helps plan a minor theft, which is ultimately not executed, may still face punishment for their role in abetting the theft.

Example 2

If an abettor assists in planning an act punishable by imprisonment, but the act is prevented or does not occur, the abettor can still be held accountable for their involvement.

Section 117: Abetting Commission of Offence by the Public or by More Than Ten Persons

Description

This section addresses the abetment of crimes involving public participation or acts committed by groups of more than ten people. It provides guidelines on the responsibility and punishment for those who instigate or facilitate such acts.

Example 1

If someone incites a large group to commit a riot or other collective crime, they may be held liable for abetting the offense under this section.

Example 2

An individual who encourages or organizes a large-scale public protest that turns violent could be considered an abettor under this section.

Section 118: Concealing Design to Commit Offence Punishable with Death or Imprisonment for Life

Description

This section addresses the concealment of the intention to commit an offense punishable with death or life imprisonment. It defines the legal consequences for hiding such intentions from authorities.

Example 1

If a person secretly plans a murder but hides their intentions, they can be prosecuted under this section for concealing their criminal design.

Example 2

An individual who keeps their plan to commit a serious crime such as a terrorist attack hidden from authorities can be charged with concealing their criminal intentions.

Section 119: Public Servant Concealing Design to Commit Offence Which It Is His Duty to Prevent

Description

This section pertains to public servants who conceal or fail to disclose the design to commit an offense that is within their duty to prevent. It sets out the penalties for public officials who neglect their responsibilities in this regard.

Example 1

A police officer who learns of a planned violent crime but does not report it or act to prevent it may be prosecuted under this section.

Example 2

A government official who is aware of a scheme to commit a serious offense, such as corruption, and chooses to conceal it rather than take preventive action, can be held liable under this section.

Section 120: Concealing Design to Commit Offence Punishable with Imprisonment

Description

This section concerns the concealment of the intention to commit an offense punishable with imprisonment. It defines the legal ramifications for individuals who hide their criminal plans.

Example 1

A person who plans a crime like theft but keeps their intentions secret can be charged under this section for concealing their criminal design.

Example 2

If someone plans a minor offense and deliberately hides their intentions from the authorities, they may be held liable under this section.

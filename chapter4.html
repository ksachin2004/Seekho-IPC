Seekho IPC - Chapter IV

Chapter IV: General Exceptions (Sections 76 to 106)

Section 76: Act Done by a Person Bound, or by Mistake of Fact Believing Himself Bound, by Law

Description

This section deals with acts performed by individuals who are legally bound to act in a certain way, or who mistakenly believe they are bound by law. Such acts are not considered criminal if performed under the belief of legal obligation.

Example 1

A public official who performs an action believing it to be their duty, even if later found to be mistaken, is not criminally liable if the act was done in good faith.

Example 2

An individual who acts under the mistaken belief that a certain action is required by law is protected from criminal liability under this section.

Section 77: Act of Judge When Acting Judicially

Description

This section addresses actions taken by judges while performing their judicial duties. Such actions are not considered criminal, provided they are done in the course of their official duties.

Example 1

A judge's decision or order in a court case, made in the course of their judicial functions, is protected under this section from criminal liability.

Example 2

Actions taken by a judge that are within the scope of their judicial duties, even if later challenged, are not deemed criminal under this section.

Section 78: Act Done Pursuant to the Judgment or Order of Court

Description

This section covers actions performed in compliance with a court's judgment or order. Such acts are not considered criminal as they are done in obedience to legal directives.

Example 1

A person executing a court order for the seizure of property is not criminally liable for the act, as it is done in accordance with judicial instructions.

Example 2

An individual following a court's directive to pay a specified amount is protected from criminal liability under this section.

Section 79: Act Done by a Person Justified, or by Mistake of Fact Believing Himself Justified, by Law

Description

This section applies to actions carried out by individuals who believe they are justified by law, or are actually justified by law, in performing the act. Such actions are not deemed criminal.

Example 1

A person who detains someone under the mistaken belief that they are authorized to do so by law, but who is actually justified by law, is not criminally liable.

Example 2

Actions performed by someone who believes, or is in fact, justified by law to act in a certain way are protected from criminal liability under this section.

Section 80: Accident in Doing a Lawful Act

Description

This section addresses accidents that occur while performing a lawful act. Such accidents are not considered criminal if the act was done lawfully and the harm was unintended.

Example 1

An individual who causes accidental harm while lawfully defending their property is not held criminally liable under this section.

Example 2

An accident that occurs during the lawful performance of a task, where no criminal intent is present, is not considered a criminal act under this section.

Section 81: Act Likely to Cause Harm, but Done Without Criminal Intent, and to Prevent Other Harm

Description

This section covers acts that may cause harm but are performed without criminal intent and with the purpose of preventing greater harm. Such acts are not considered criminal under this section.

Example 1

A person who breaks a window to save someone from a fire, even though it causes damage, is not criminally liable if the act was done to prevent greater harm.

Example 2

Actions taken to prevent imminent harm, even if they cause some degree of harm, are protected from criminal liability if done with the intent to prevent greater damage.

Section 82: Act of a Child Under Seven Years of Age

Description

This section specifies that a child under the age of seven is not considered criminally responsible for their actions. The act of such a child is not deemed criminal.

Example 1

A child below seven years old cannot be held criminally liable for any actions they undertake, as they are presumed incapable of forming criminal intent.

Example 2

Acts committed by children under the age of seven are not subject to criminal charges due to their age and presumed lack of criminal capacity.

Section 83: Act of a Child Above Seven and Under Twelve of Immature Understanding

Description

This section addresses the criminal liability of children aged between seven and twelve who have an immature understanding. Such children are only held criminally responsible if they are found to have an understanding of the nature and consequences of their actions.

Example 1

A child between seven and twelve years old may be held criminally liable if it is proven that they had an understanding of the wrongful nature of their act.

Example 2

Children in this age group are assessed for their ability to comprehend the implications of their actions before being held criminally responsible.

Section 84: Act of a Person of Unsound Mind

Description

This section provides that an act committed by a person who is of unsound mind is not considered criminal if they were incapable of understanding the nature of their actions due to their mental condition.

Example 1

A person with a severe mental disorder who commits an act while not understanding the nature of their actions is not held criminally responsible under this section.

Example 2

Individuals deemed to be of unsound mind at the time of committing an act are not subject to criminal liability if their mental state impaired their understanding of the act's criminality.

Section 85: Act of a Person Incapable of Judgment by Reason of Intoxication Caused Against His Will

Description

This section applies to individuals who are unable to form judgment or intent due to intoxication that was caused against their will. Such individuals are not held criminally liable for their actions under this condition.

Example 1

An individual who is drugged without their consent and commits an act while intoxicated is not criminally liable if they were incapable of forming judgment due to the forced intoxication.

Example 2

If someone is involuntarily intoxicated and commits an act while unable to understand its criminal nature, they are not held criminally responsible under this section.

Section 86: Offence Requiring a Particular Intent or Knowledge Committed by One Who is Intoxicated

Description

This section deals with offences that require a specific intent or knowledge. If an individual who is intoxicated commits such an offence, the fact of their intoxication does not excuse them from liability.

Example 1

An individual who, while intoxicated, commits an offence that requires specific intent, such as premeditated murder, is not excused from liability due to their intoxication.

Example 2

Intoxication does not negate criminal liability for offences that require particular knowledge or intent, even if the intoxicated state impairs judgment.

Section 87: Act Not Intended and Not Known to be Likely to Cause Death or Grievous Hurt, Done by Consent

Description

This section provides that an act which is not intended to cause death or grievous hurt, and which the perpetrator did not know was likely to cause such harm, is not considered criminal if done with the consent of the person affected.

Example 1

If a person performs an act that unintentionally causes harm, but was done with the consent of the injured party and without knowledge that it would cause serious harm, it is not considered criminal.

Example 2

Acts performed with consent that are not intended or known to be likely to cause serious injury are protected from criminal liability under this section.

Section 88: Act Not Intended to Cause Death, Done by Consent in Good Faith for Person’s Benefit

Description

This section covers acts done with the consent of the affected person, not intended to cause death, and performed in good faith for the benefit of that person. Such acts are not considered criminal.

Example 1

A medical procedure performed with the consent of the patient, intended to benefit their health but not to cause death, is not criminal under this section.

Example 2

Acts done in good faith for the benefit of others, where the intention is not to cause death and consent is provided, are not deemed criminal.

Section 89: Act Done in Good Faith for Benefit of Child or Insane Person, by or by Consent of Guardian

Description

This section provides that acts done in good faith for the benefit of a child or a person of unsound mind, either by the person themselves or with the consent of their guardian, are not considered criminal.

Example 1

A medical treatment given to a child or a person with a mental disorder, done with the consent of their guardian and for their benefit, is not held criminally liable under this section.

Example 2

Acts that are performed in good faith to benefit children or mentally unsound individuals, with appropriate consent, are exempt from criminal liability.

Section 90: Consent Known to be Given Under Fear or Misconception

Description

This section specifies that consent given under fear of injury or under a misconception of fact is not considered valid consent. Therefore, acts performed under such consent are not protected.

Example 1

If consent is obtained from someone under duress or based on false information, it does not constitute valid consent, and the act performed is not protected.

Example 2

Consent given under pressure or false pretenses does not shield the act from being considered criminal.

Section 91: Exclusion of Acts Which Are Offences Independently of Harm Caused

Description

This section provides that acts which are considered offences regardless of the harm they cause are excluded from being justified under any general exceptions. The nature of the act itself renders it criminal.

Example 1

Certain acts, such as bribery or forgery, are criminal offenses irrespective of whether they result in any direct harm.

Example 2

Acts that are inherently criminal by their nature, such as illegal possession of arms, are not justified by general exceptions of harm caused.

Section 92: Act Done in Good Faith for Benefit of a Person Without Consent

Description

This section covers acts done in good faith for the benefit of a person without their consent. Such acts are not considered criminal if they are performed with the intention of benefiting the person and without any intention of causing harm.

Example 1

A medical intervention performed on an unconscious person to save their life, even without their consent, is not criminal if done in good faith.

Example 2

Acts carried out in an emergency to benefit someone, even if consent cannot be obtained, are not criminal if performed with good intentions.

Section 93: Communication Made in Good Faith

Description

This section states that communications made in good faith are not considered criminal, even if they are erroneous or misleading, provided they are not intended to cause harm.

Example 1

A person who gives incorrect information to a friend with the intention of helping them, but which turns out to be misleading, is not criminally liable if it was done in good faith.

Example 2

Communications that are intended to be helpful, even if they are mistaken or inaccurate, do not constitute a criminal offense if made in good faith.

Section 94: Act to Which a Person is Compelled by Threats

Description

This section provides that acts committed under the compulsion of threats are not considered criminal if the threats were of such a nature that they compelled the person to act against their will.

Example 1

A person who commits a crime because they were threatened with harm to themselves or their loved ones may be exempt from criminal liability if the threats were severe enough to compel their actions.

Example 2

Acts performed under significant threats that leave no reasonable choice but to comply are not criminal if the threats were sufficient to compel the action.

Section 95: Act Causing Slight Harm

Description

This section specifies that acts which cause only slight harm are not considered criminal if the harm caused is minimal and does not result in significant injury.

Example 1

A minor injury or insignificant harm that does not lead to any substantial impact is not subject to criminal charges under this section.

Example 2

Acts resulting in trivial harm that does not cause any significant damage or distress are excluded from criminal liability.

Section 96: Things Done in Private Defence

Description

This section provides that things done in the exercise of the right of private defence are not considered criminal. It recognizes that acts performed in self-defence or the defence of property are legally protected.

Example 1

A person who uses reasonable force to protect themselves from an attacker is not held criminally liable for their actions, as long as the force used was in legitimate self-defence.

Example 2

Acts performed to defend one’s property from theft or damage, using necessary force, are not considered criminal if done within the bounds of private defence.

Section 97: Right of Private Defence of the Body and of Property

Description

This section elaborates on the right to private defence, specifying that a person has the right to defend their body and property from harm. The extent of this right is detailed in subsequent sections.

Example 1

A person can use reasonable force to prevent a burglary in their home or to protect themselves from physical assault.

Example 2

If someone is attacked, they can defend themselves with appropriate force, and this action will be justified under the right of private defence.

Section 98: Right of Private Defence Against the Act of a Person of Unsound Mind, etc.

Description

This section specifies that the right of private defence also extends against acts committed by individuals who are of unsound mind or otherwise incapacitated. The protection remains valid in such cases.

Example 1

If a person with a mental illness attacks someone, the victim may defend themselves using necessary force, as the right of private defence applies.

Example 2

A person who is attacked by someone who is not in full control of their actions due to a medical condition may still use reasonable force in self-defence.

Section 99: Act Against Which There is No Right of Private Defence

Description

This section defines circumstances where the right of private defence does not apply. It clarifies that certain acts are not protected by the right of private defence and may be subject to legal consequences.

Example 1

If someone is provoked into a fight, the right of private defence might not apply if the initial act of provocation was not itself unlawful.

Example 2

If a person uses force in retaliation for a minor insult, rather than an immediate threat, this may not be protected under the right of private defence.

Section 100: When the Right of Private Defence of the Body Extends to Causing Death

Description

This section outlines the conditions under which the right of private defence of the body extends to causing death. It specifies the severity of threat required for the use of lethal force to be justified.

Example 1

A person facing an imminent threat of death may use lethal force to defend themselves, and this action would be justified under the right of private defence.

Example 2

If an attacker is armed and threatens to kill, using deadly force to prevent the attack is legally justified under the right of private defence.

Section 101: When Such Right Extends to Causing Any Harm Other Than Death

Description

This section details situations where the right of private defence allows for causing harm other than death. It specifies circumstances where non-lethal force may be used to defend oneself or others.

Example 1

If someone is being attacked and causing injury to the attacker is sufficient to stop the attack, this right extends to causing harm other than death.

Example 2

Using non-lethal force to incapacitate an attacker who poses a threat but is not armed with deadly weapons is permissible under this section.

Section 102: Commencement and Continuance of the Right of Private Defence of the Body

Description

This section explains when the right of private defence of the body begins and how long it lasts. It outlines the conditions under which this right is applicable and its duration.

Example 1

The right of private defence commences when a threat is imminent and continues as long as the threat persists, allowing the person to defend themselves for the duration of the threat.

Example 2

If an attack is ongoing, the right of private defence lasts until the threat is neutralized or the attacker retreats.

Section 103: When the Right of Private Defence of Property Extends to Causing Death

Description

This section defines the circumstances under which the right to private defence of property can extend to causing death. It outlines the conditions where lethal force may be used to protect property.

Example 1

If someone is breaking into a home and poses a threat to the occupants, the owner may use deadly force to protect their property if the situation warrants it.

Example 2

The right to use lethal force extends if there is an imminent threat to the life of the property owner or occupants while protecting their property.

Section 104: When Such Right Extends to Causing Any Harm Other Than Death

Description

This section describes when the right of private defence of property extends to causing harm other than death. It provides guidelines on the use of non-lethal force to protect property.

Example 1

Using physical force to prevent a theft or damage to property, without causing death, is justified under this section if it is necessary to protect the property.

Example 2

If someone is attempting to vandalize property, the property owner may use reasonable force to stop the vandalism, as long as it does not result in death.

Section 105: Commencement and Continuance of the Right of Private Defence of Property

Description

This section outlines when the right of private defence of property begins and ends. It provides details on the duration of this right and the conditions under which it applies.

Example 1

The right of private defence of property begins when an attack on the property is imminent and continues until the threat is neutralized or the attacker has retreated.

Example 2

The right to defend property continues as long as the attack is ongoing, allowing the property owner to use necessary force to prevent further damage.

Section 106: Right of Private Defence Against Deadly Assault When There Is Risk of Harm to Innocent Person

Description

This section allows for the right of private defence against a deadly assault even when there is a risk of harm to an innocent person. It provides conditions under which such actions are justified to protect innocent lives.

Example 1

If an attacker poses a deadly threat to multiple people, including innocent bystanders, the right to use force to stop the attacker is justified, even if there is some risk of collateral harm.

Example 2

In a situation where a person needs to use deadly force to protect others from a deadly assault, the right of private defence extends to taking such action despite the potential risk to bystanders.

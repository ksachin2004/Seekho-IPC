Seekho IPC - Chapter II

Chapter II: General Explanations (Sections 6 to 52A)

Section 6: Definitions in the Code to Be Understood Subject to Exceptions

Description

This section outlines that definitions provided in the Code should be understood with the consideration of any exceptions specified within the Code. It ensures that general definitions are applied appropriately with due regard to exceptions.

Example 1

If the Code defines "property" in a general sense but includes exceptions for certain types of property, those exceptions must be considered when interpreting the term "property."

Example 2

A general definition of "offense" might include exceptions for specific offenses that are covered under separate statutes or regulations.

Section 7: Sense of Expression Once Explained

Description

This section ensures that terms defined or explained once will carry the same meaning throughout the Code unless explicitly stated otherwise.

Example 1

If the term "property" is defined in one section, it will hold the same meaning wherever it appears unless otherwise specified.

Example 2

The term "person" once defined will apply consistently throughout the IPC unless a different definition is provided.

Section 8: Gender

Description

This section provides that words referring to the masculine gender include the feminine gender, and vice versa, unless the context requires otherwise.

Example 1

If a section refers to "he" in a legal context, it is understood to include "she" as well, ensuring gender-neutral interpretation where applicable.

Example 2

A term like "offender" will include both male and female individuals, adhering to the principle of gender inclusivity.

Section 9: Number

Description

This section states that words denoting the singular number include the plural number, and vice versa, unless the context otherwise requires.

Example 1

A term such as "person" is understood to refer to multiple individuals if the context implies plurality.

Example 2

References to "a witness" may include "witnesses" if the situation involves more than one individual.

Section 10: Man, Woman

Description

This section clarifies that terms "man" and "woman" are used to denote human beings of the respective genders, and should be understood as such in the context of the Code.

Example 1

The term "man" in a legal context includes both males and females, reflecting an inclusive understanding.

Example 2

The reference to "woman" is interpreted as encompassing female individuals in the legal context.

Section 11: Person

Description

This section defines "person" as including any individual, corporation, company, or body of persons, whether incorporated or not.

Example 1

A "person" under the Code could be a private individual or a corporate entity involved in legal proceedings.

Example 2

The term "person" also includes non-corporate bodies, such as partnerships or associations, in legal contexts.

Section 12: Public

Description

This section defines "public" as referring to a group of individuals, whether specific or general, and is not limited to any particular segment of society.

Example 1

"Public" can refer to the general populace or to a specific group that is open to or accessible by everyone.

Example 2

A public meeting or public space is one that is open to all members of society without restriction.

Section 13: Queen

Description

This section defines "Queen" as referring to the reigning monarch or the head of state recognized as the queen in the context of the legal framework.

Example 1

In legal documents, "Queen" refers to the current monarch, whose roles and duties are defined by the constitution or legal code.

Example 2

Historical references to "Queen" pertain to the reigning queen of the time of the document's creation.

Section 14: Servant of Government

Description

This section defines "servant of government" as an individual employed by or serving the government in an official capacity, including officers and employees.

Example 1

A civil servant or government official falls under the definition of "servant of government," given their role in public administration.

Example 2

Employees of government agencies or departments who carry out governmental functions are considered servants of the government.

Section 15: British India

Description

This section defines "British India" as the territories under the administration of the British Crown in India before independence, excluding princely states.

Example 1

References to "British India" in legal contexts pertain to the territories governed directly by the British Crown, excluding areas with autonomous princely rulers.

Example 2

Historical laws and regulations that applied to British India are relevant for understanding the legal context before India's independence in 1947.

Section 16: Government of India

Description

This section defines "Government of India" as the central government of India, including all its branches, departments, and officials.

Example 1

References to the "Government of India" include the President, Prime Minister, and all ministries and departments functioning under the central government.

Example 2

Legal documents referring to the "Government of India" encompass legislative, executive, and judicial functions of the central government.

Section 17: Government

Description

This section defines "Government" as the authority that governs a region, including its executive, legislative, and judicial branches, and other related entities.

Example 1

The term "Government" includes the central government and state governments, as well as their respective agencies and departments.

Example 2

References to "Government" in legal contexts cover all branches of government and their official functions.

Section 18: India

Description

This section defines "India" as the territory that constitutes the Republic of India, including all its states and territories as recognized by the Constitution.

Example 1

In legal documents, "India" refers to the entire geographic and political entity as per the boundaries defined in the Constitution.

Example 2

References to "India" include all states, union territories, and any territory that may be added to the Republic of India in the future.

Section 19: Judge

Description

This section defines "Judge" as an individual appointed to preside over legal proceedings, including those who perform judicial functions in various courts.

Example 1

A "Judge" includes judges of the Supreme Court, High Courts, and lower courts who are responsible for adjudicating legal disputes.

Example 2

In legal contexts, the term "Judge" refers to any person who has been given judicial authority to make legal decisions.

Section 20: Court of Justice

Description

This section defines "Court of Justice" as any court established under the law for administering justice and resolving legal disputes.

Example 1

A "Court of Justice" includes the Supreme Court, High Courts, District Courts, and any other judicial bodies recognized by law.

Example 2

The term encompasses any forum where legal matters are adjudicated, including specialized courts or tribunals.

Section 21: Public Servant

Description

This section defines "Public Servant" as any individual who serves the public in an official capacity, including government employees and officials.

Example 1

A "Public Servant" includes civil servants, police officers, and other government employees who perform duties for the public.

Example 2

The term applies to anyone holding a public office or position of authority, regardless of their specific role or agency.

Section 22: Moveable Property

Description

This section defines "Moveable Property" as property that can be moved from one place to another, including tangible and intangible assets.

Example 1

Examples of moveable property include vehicles, furniture, and electronic devices.

Example 2

Intangible moveable property such as shares or bonds also falls under this definition, as they can be transferred between parties.

Section 23: Wrongful Gain

Description

This section defines "Wrongful Gain" as gaining something through unlawful means or by deceit, which is not legally entitled to the individual.

Example 1

If a person acquires property through fraud or misrepresentation, it is considered a wrongful gain.

Example 2

Receiving funds through illegal activities, such as bribery or embezzlement, constitutes wrongful gain.

Section 24: Dishonestly

Description

This section defines "Dishonestly" as acting with the intention of deceiving or cheating, which results in unlawful gain or loss.

Example 1

Dishonest behavior includes falsifying documents or making false claims to obtain financial benefits.

Example 2

Engaging in fraudulent activities with the intent to deceive others is an example of acting dishonestly.

Section 25: Fraudulently

Description

This section defines "Fraudulently" as performing an act with the intention of deceiving another person or entity, usually for personal gain.

Example 1

Fraudulent activities include falsifying information to obtain a loan or making false statements in a legal document.

Example 2

Creating fake identities or documents to deceive others falls under fraudulent actions.

Section 26: Reason to Believe

Description

This section defines "Reason to Believe" as having a justification or valid grounds for forming a belief, which must be based on credible evidence or circumstances.

Example 1

If an officer has credible information about a crime, they have a reason to believe that an investigation is warranted.

Example 2

In a legal case, having substantial evidence to support an accusation gives one reason to believe in its validity.

Section 27: Property in Possession of Wife, Clerk, or Servant

Description

This section defines the property held by a wife, clerk, or servant on behalf of another person, typically their employer or head of the household.

Example 1

Property owned by a husband but physically possessed by his wife is considered as property in the possession of the wife.

Example 2

A clerk holding company documents or a servant possessing valuables for their employer fall under this definition.

Section 28: Counterfeit

Description

This section defines "Counterfeit" as the act of creating imitation or fake items intended to deceive others, especially to obtain unlawful benefits.

Example 1

Counterfeiting currency involves making fake money with the intent to pass it as genuine legal tender.

Example 2

Creating imitation trademarks or logos to mislead consumers about the origin of goods is also considered counterfeiting.

Section 29: Document

Description

This section defines "Document" as any written or printed material that records information, evidence, or transactions, which can be used for legal purposes.

Example 1

Legal contracts, certificates, and official correspondence are examples of documents.

Example 2

Documents also include records like birth certificates, deeds, and affidavits.

Section 29A: Electronic Record

Description

This section defines "Electronic Record" as information or data stored in electronic form, which can be accessed, stored, or transmitted electronically.

Example 1

Emails, digital files, and electronic documents are types of electronic records.

Example 2

Records maintained in electronic databases or cloud storage also fall under this definition.

Section 30: Valuable Security

Description

This section defines "Valuable Security" as any document or instrument that represents a right to property or an interest of value, which can be used to secure a debt or obligation.

Example 1

Examples of valuable securities include promissory notes, bonds, and certificates of deposit.

Example 2

Shares in a corporation or any document that represents ownership or interest in property are considered valuable securities.

Section 31: A Will

Description

This section defines "A Will" as a legal document by which a person expresses their wishes regarding the distribution of their property and assets after their death.

Example 1

A will can specify how a person's estate should be divided among their heirs or beneficiaries.

Example 2

It may also include instructions for the care of minor children or the appointment of an executor to manage the estate.

Section 32: Words Referring to Acts Include Illegal Omissions

Description

This section explains that words referring to "acts" include illegal omissions, meaning that failing to perform a required act can be considered an offense under the law.

Example 1

If a law requires reporting a financial transaction and an individual fails to do so, the omission is treated as a criminal act.

Example 2

Failure to provide necessary medical care when legally required is considered an illegal omission under this section.

Section 33: Act Omission

Description

This section defines "Act Omission" as the failure to perform an act that is required by law or by duty, which can result in criminal liability if it leads to harm or constitutes a breach of legal obligations.

Example 1

A person's omission to act, such as failing to report child abuse when mandated, can result in legal consequences.

Example 2

Not complying with safety regulations in a workplace, leading to harm, is considered an omission of duty.

Section 34: Acts Done by Several Persons in Furtherance of Common Intention

Description

This section defines acts done by several persons with a common intention as actions performed by multiple individuals who share the same goal or purpose, leading to collective criminal liability.

Example 1

If a group plans and commits a robbery together, all participants are liable for the crime due to their common intention.

Example 2

In cases of conspiracy, where multiple individuals act together to achieve an unlawful objective, they are collectively responsible for their actions.

Section 35: When Such an Act is Criminal by Reason of its Being Done with Criminal Knowledge or Intention

Description

This section defines when an act is considered criminal due to the individual's criminal knowledge or intention, meaning that the crime is aggravated or determined by the perpetrator’s awareness and intent.

Example 1

Committing a crime with premeditation or intent to cause harm escalates the offense to a more serious crime, such as murder instead of manslaughter.

Example 2

An act of theft carried out with the knowledge that it is unlawful, and with the intention to permanently deprive the owner of their property, is a criminal offense.

Section 36: Effect Caused Partly by Act and Partly by Omission

Description

This section addresses situations where an effect results from both an act and an omission. It recognizes that both actions and failures to act can contribute to the occurrence of a consequence.

Example 1

If a person administers poison (act) but the victim's condition worsens because the person failed to seek medical help (omission), both the act and omission contribute to the outcome.

Example 2

A building collapse caused by faulty construction (act) and the failure to repair known defects (omission) results in injury or damage.

Section 37: Co-operation by Doing One of Several Acts Constituting an Offence

Description

This section covers the legal responsibility of individuals who contribute to an offense by performing one of several acts that together constitute a crime. Each person’s contribution is considered criminal.

Example 1

In a theft ring, one person might break into a building, another might disable security systems, and a third might remove the stolen goods. Each participant is liable for the crime.

Example 2

During a robbery, if one individual restrains the victim, another carries out the theft, and a third acts as a lookout, all are complicit in the criminal act.

Section 38: Persons Concerned in Criminal Act May Be Guilty of Different Offences

Description

This section acknowledges that individuals involved in a criminal act may be charged with different offenses based on their level of involvement and the nature of their actions.

Example 1

In a bank robbery, the mastermind, the person who executes the robbery, and the getaway driver may each be charged with different offenses related to their roles.

Example 2

During a drug trafficking operation, individuals involved in manufacturing, distributing, and selling drugs can face different charges according to their specific contributions.

Section 39: Voluntarily

Description

This section defines "Voluntarily" as actions taken by an individual with intention or consent, where the person has a choice and willingly engages in the conduct.

Example 1

If a person decides to assist in a crime knowing the consequences, their involvement is considered voluntary.

Example 2

Voluntarily making a confession or admitting to an offense in court, knowing the legal implications, is an example of voluntary action.

Section 40: Offence

Description

This section defines "Offence" as any act or omission that is punishable by law. It covers both criminal and civil offenses as defined by statutory provisions.

Example 1

Theft, assault, and fraud are examples of offenses that result in legal penalties or punishments.

Example 2

Traffic violations such as speeding or running a red light are considered offenses under traffic laws.

Section 41: Special Law

Description

This section defines "Special Law" as any law that is specific to certain circumstances or groups, which overrides general provisions in particular cases.

Example 1

Special laws such as the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act provide specific regulations and penalties for drug-related offenses.

Example 2

Laws governing intellectual property rights serve as special laws providing protection to creators and inventors against infringement.

Section 42: Local Law

Description

This section defines "Local Law" as any legislation that applies specifically to a certain geographical area or jurisdiction, as opposed to national or federal laws.

Example 1

Municipal ordinances regulating zoning and land use are examples of local laws.

Example 2

Local traffic regulations that vary from city to city are also considered local laws.

Section 43: Illegal, Legally Bound to Do

Description

This section explains that "Illegal" refers to actions that are against the law, while being "Legally Bound to Do" implies obligations imposed by law that must be fulfilled.

Example 1

Failing to file tax returns as required by law is considered illegal and is an example of being legally bound to fulfill an obligation.

Example 2

A business operating without a required license is engaging in illegal activity and is legally bound to obtain the license.

Section 44: Injury

Description

This section defines "Injury" as harm or damage caused to a person, property, or interest, which can be physical, mental, or financial.

Example 1

Physical injury such as a broken bone resulting from an accident is considered an injury.

Example 2

Mental or emotional distress caused by defamation or harassment is also classified as an injury.

Section 45: Life

Description

This section defines "Life" as the existence of a human being from birth to death. It emphasizes the legal recognition of life as a fundamental right and protection under the law.

Example 1

Legal provisions protecting human life from harm or unlawful actions reflect the importance of safeguarding life.

Example 2

Laws related to homicide or bodily harm highlight the legal acknowledgment of life and its protection.

Section 46: Death

Description

This section defines "Death" as the cessation of life, which is recognized as a significant legal and social event affecting rights, obligations, and legal proceedings.

Example 1

Legal procedures for handling estates and wills after an individual's death reflect the legal implications of death.

Example 2

Criminal laws addressing murder or manslaughter pertain to the unlawful taking of life, highlighting the legal recognition of death.

Section 47: Animal

Description

This section defines "Animal" as any living creature other than humans. It includes mammals, birds, reptiles, and other species, and recognizes the legal implications related to animals.

Example 1

Laws concerning animal cruelty or protection relate to the treatment of animals under legal provisions.

Example 2

Regulations on the care and management of domestic pets fall under legal definitions of "Animal."

Section 48: Vessel

Description

This section defines "Vessel" as any type of watercraft used for navigation on water, including ships, boats, and submarines. It encompasses legal regulations pertaining to such vessels.

Example 1

Maritime laws governing the operation and safety of ships and boats fall under this definition.

Example 2

Regulations on vessel registration, navigation, and environmental protection are covered under the definition of "Vessel."

Section 49: Year, Month

Description

This section defines "Year" and "Month" as units of time used in legal contexts to measure durations and deadlines. A year consists of 12 months, and each month typically has between 28 and 31 days.

Example 1

Statutory deadlines or periods for legal actions may be measured in years or months, such as the statute of limitations for filing a lawsuit.

Example 2

Contractual agreements may specify durations in months or years, affecting the performance or enforcement of the contract.

Section 50: Section

Description

This section defines "Section" as a specific subdivision within a legal statute or document. It helps to locate and refer to particular provisions within the law.

Example 1

A legal statute may be divided into sections to organize and reference various provisions, such as Section 10 and Section 20 of a criminal code.

Example 2

Legal documents and contracts often use sections to divide content, making it easier to locate specific clauses or terms.

Section 51: Oath

Description

This section defines "Oath" as a solemn declaration or promise, often invoking a higher power or authority, made to affirm the truthfulness of statements or commitments.

Example 1

Witnesses in court are required to take an oath to tell the truth before giving testimony.

Example 2

Public officials may take an oath of office, pledging to uphold the constitution and perform their duties faithfully.

Section 52: Good Faith

Description

This section defines "Good Faith" as acting with honesty, integrity, and fairness in dealings and transactions. It implies an absence of deceit or fraud.

Example 1

Negotiating a contract in good faith involves making honest disclosures and not hiding any material facts.

Example 2

Performing fiduciary duties with good faith means acting in the best interests of those whom you represent, such as a trustee managing assets for beneficiaries.

Section 52A: Harbour

Description

This section defines "Harbour" as providing refuge, support, or shelter to someone or something, often in the context of aiding or concealing individuals or objects.

Example 1

Providing shelter to a person fleeing from the law can be considered harbouring.

Example 2

Concealing stolen property or aiding criminals can also fall under the definition of harbouring.
